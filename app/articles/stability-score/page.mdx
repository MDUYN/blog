# A Stability Score for Bounded Trading Metrics

<div className="text-sm text-[var(--color-terminal-text-dim)] mt-2 mb-8">
  April 2026
</div>
</div>

In [the previous article](/articles/consistency-score) I described the consistency score the [Investing Algorithm Framework](https://github.com/coding-kitties/investing-algorithm-framework) uses to measure how reliably a strategy reproduces its performance across walk-forward windows. That score is built on the coefficient of variation (CV), which is scale-invariant and works well for unbounded metrics like returns.

But CV has a sharp edge: **it falls apart when the mean is near zero**, and it gives counter-intuitive answers for metrics that live on a known, bounded scale. Win rate is naturally between 0 and 100. Sharpe ratio is meaningfully bounded in practice, somewhere around -2 to +4. For these, a different formulation works better.

That's the **stability score**.

The picture above is the whole argument in one frame. Both strategies have the same per-window standard deviation (σ ≈ 5). CV says they're wildly different (0.90 versus 0.00) because Strategy B's mean is small. Stability says they're identically tight, which is what you actually want to know about a bounded metric.

## Why a second score, and why not reuse existing ones?

Once I had the consistency score working, the obvious question was: _why not just use it for everything?_ The answer is that CV-based scoring quietly produces nonsense for bounded metrics, and the existing alternatives didn't fit either.

- **[`pyfolio.stability_of_timeseries`](https://github.com/quantopian/pyfolio/blob/master/pyfolio/timeseries.py)** is the most-cited "stability" metric in open-source quant code. It's the R² of a linear fit to cumulative log returns, i.e. _how straight is the equity curve?_ That's a useful number, but it operates on the equity curve itself, not on per-window aggregate metrics like win rate or Sharpe. You can't apply it to "the win rates of these 12 walk-forward windows."
- **CV / consistency score** breaks down when the mean is small relative to the natural scale of the metric. Win rates of `1%, 2%, 3%` have a tiny absolute spread but a huge CV. The score reports "wildly inconsistent" when the strategy is in fact _reliably losing in the same way every window_. That's the wrong answer for a bounded metric.
- **[Deflated Sharpe Ratio](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1821643)** is rigorous but produces a probability around a single Sharpe value. It doesn't generalize to "stability of win rate" or "stability of profitable-window ratio."
- **[Walk-Forward Efficiency](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Evaluation+and+Optimization+of+Trading+Strategies%2C+2nd+Edition-p-9780470128015)** is a ratio of OOS to IS performance. It says nothing about how much that OOS performance wobbles window-to-window.

What I actually wanted for bounded metrics was direct: pick a sensible "very unstable" reference (`σ_max`) for that metric, scale the observed σ against it, and put the result in `[0, 1]`. No mean in the denominator, no edge cases at zero, no R² fits, no probability theory. Just _how tightly do the per-window values cluster, on a scale you can interpret without a footnote?_

That's what the stability score is, and it only exists because no off-the-shelf metric handled bounded per-window aggregates the way I needed for the framework's backtest report.

## Normalized standard deviation

Instead of normalizing σ by the mean, the stability score normalizes by a **domain-specific maximum standard deviation**: `stability = 1 - σ / σ_max`.

```python
def get_normalized_stability(values, max_std):
    if len(values) < 2:
        return None
    mean = sum(values) / len(values)
    var = sum((x - mean) ** 2 for x in values) / (len(values) - 1)
    std = math.sqrt(var)
    return max(0.0, min(1.0, 1.0 - std / max_std))
```

The result lives in `[0, 1]` and has a direct interpretation: a stability of `0.9` means the per-window standard deviation is 10% of what you've decided "very unstable" looks like for this metric.

## Why this is better for bounded metrics

Take win rate, which is bounded to `[0, 100]`. Suppose a strategy has per-window win rates of `45, 50, 55`. The mean is 50, σ ≈ 5, CV = 0.1. So the CV-based score reports a consistency of `0.9`.

Now consider a strategy with per-window win rates of `1, 2, 3`. Same shape, same σ, but a tiny mean. CV = 5/2 = 2.5, so the consistency score reports `0.0`. Maximally inconsistent.

That's mathematically correct, but practically the second strategy is also _maximally consistent in the way it loses_. The win rate doesn't fluctuate, it's just low. CV is the wrong tool here because the mean is the denominator and the meaningful scale is set externally, not by the data itself.

The stability score sidesteps this by anchoring to a fixed `max_std` chosen for the metric:

- **Win rate**: `max_std` ≈ 25 (a strategy whose win rate moves ±25 percentage points window-to-window is wildly unstable).
- **Sharpe ratio**: `max_std` ≈ 1.5.
- **Returns**: stability is _less_ useful here because there's no natural ceiling. Stick with the CV-based consistency.

Both `45, 50, 55` and `1, 2, 3` get the same stability score, because the _spread_ is what's being measured, not the spread relative to the mean. That's the right answer for win rate.

## The composite

Stability composes the same way consistency does: weighted by component, with the same 35/25/20/20 split for returns, win rate, Sharpe, and profitable-window ratio.

```python
def get_stability_score(
    return_stability,
    win_rate_stability,
    sharpe_stability,
    number_of_profitable_windows=None,
    number_of_windows=None,
):
    return _composite(
        return_stability,
        win_rate_stability,
        sharpe_stability,
        number_of_profitable_windows,
        number_of_windows,
    )
```

Internally both scores share `_composite()`. Only the per-component normalization differs. That keeps the two scores comparable: a stability of 0.8 and a consistency of 0.8 mean the same _shape_ of result, just measured against different references.

## Consistency vs stability: when to use which

Both scores answer the same question: _how reliable is this strategy across windows?_ But they answer it through different lenses:

| Score | Normalization | Best for | Watch out for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Consistency | σ / |μ| (CV) | Returns, P&L, anything unbounded | Mean ≈ 0 collapses the score to 0 |
| Stability | σ / σ\_max | Win rate, Sharpe, bounded metrics | Requires a sensible `max_std` per metric |

In practice the framework computes **both** and exposes them in the backtest report. They tend to agree on great strategies and bad strategies. Where they disagree is informative, and usually the disagreement points at something interesting in the underlying distributions, like a strategy whose returns are tight but whose win rates swing wildly.

## Prior art

The word "stability" is overloaded in quant trading. A few specific things people already mean by it, none of which are quite this:

- **`pyfolio.timeseries.stability_of_timeseries`**. Quantopian's pyfolio shipped a metric literally called _stability_, defined as the R² of a linear fit to cumulative log returns. Same name, very different formulation. [pyfolio source](https://github.com/quantopian/pyfolio/blob/master/pyfolio/timeseries.py).
- **Probabilistic / Deflated Sharpe Ratio** in [Bailey & López de Prado, 2014](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=1821643). Treats Sharpe-ratio stability as a probability of exceeding a threshold given sample size and higher moments.
- **Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation (CPCV)** by Marcos López de Prado in [_Advances in Financial Machine Learning_ (2018)](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Advances+in+Financial+Machine+Learning-p-9781119482086). Builds a distribution of Sharpe ratios over many train/test combinations and looks at its spread.
- **Walk-Forward Efficiency** by Robert Pardo in [_The Evaluation and Optimization of Trading Strategies_ (2008)](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/The+Evaluation+and+Optimization+of+Trading+Strategies%2C+2nd+Edition-p-9780470128015). Different formulation again, but the same underlying question.
- **Coefficient of variation**, the basis of the [companion consistency score](/articles/consistency-score). [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient_of_variation).

What's new here is small but, I think, useful: explicitly **routing each metric to the right normalizer** (CV for unbounded things like returns, normalized σ for bounded things like win rate and Sharpe) and reporting both scores side-by-side in the same `[0, 1]` scale so they're directly comparable.

## A note on `None`

Both scores propagate `None` cleanly. Fewer than two windows means no score. A missing component is dropped from the composite and the remaining weights are renormalized. This matters more than it sounds: backtests don't always produce every metric for every window, and having the score quietly degrade rather than crash is a small but important quality-of-life improvement.

If you're already using walk-forward backtests in the framework, both scores show up in your report automatically. The implementation is in pure Python, no dependencies, and small enough to read in one sitting.
