# Building a Blotter System for the Investing Algorithm Framework

<div className="text-sm text-[var(--color-terminal-text-dim)] mt-2 mb-8">
  June 2025
</div>
</div>

If you've ever backtested a trading strategy and thought _"these results look too good"_, you're probably right. Most backtesting engines assume perfect fills. Your order goes in at exactly the price you specified, with zero fees, and the market has infinite liquidity. That's not how it works.

I just merged a [blotter system](https://github.com/coding-kitties/investing-algorithm-framework/pull/474) into the [Investing Algorithm Framework](https://github.com/coding-kitties/investing-algorithm-framework) that fixes this. Here's how it works and why it matters.

## What is a blotter?

In traditional finance, a blotter is the record of all trades made during a session, the trade diary. In the context of an algorithmic trading framework, it's more than that. It's the layer that sits between your strategy and order execution:

<div className="my-8 flex justify-center">
  <Img src="/assets/blotter-architecture.svg" alt="Blotter architecture diagram showing order flow from Strategy through Blotter to OrderService, OrderExecutor, and Exchange or Simulation" className="w-full max-w-[720px]" />
</div>

Every order, whether it's a market buy, limit sell, or a batch of rebalancing trades, flows through the blotter before hitting the order service. This gives you a single interception point to apply slippage, calculate commissions, record transactions, and even implement custom order routing.

## The design

I wanted the blotter to be:

1. **Composable.** Swap out slippage and commission models independently.
2. **Extensible.** Subclass `Blotter` for custom routing logic (e.g. smart order routing, TWAP).
3. **Transparent.** Record every fill as a `Transaction` for post-hoc analysis.
4. **Zero-config.** It just works out of the box, no setup required.

The framework auto-selects the right blotter based on the execution mode:

| Mode | Default Blotter | Behavior |
|------|----------------|----------|
| Live trading | `DefaultBlotter` | Pass-through, no slippage or commission |
| Backtesting | `SimulationBlotter` | Configurable slippage, commission, and fill models |

If you want control, call `app.set_blotter(...)` with any `Blotter` subclass.

## Slippage models

Slippage is the difference between the price you expect and the price you actually get. In a backtest, you need to model this or your results are lying to you.

The framework ships with three built-in models.

**NoSlippage.** Orders fill at the exact intended price. This is the default, and it's fine if you're just prototyping.

**PercentageSlippage.** Buy orders fill slightly higher, sell orders slightly lower. A `0.1%` slippage on a buy at `100.0` fills at `100.10`:

```python
app.set_blotter(SimulationBlotter(
    slippage_model=PercentageSlippage(percentage=0.001)
))
```

**FixedSlippage.** Adds or subtracts a fixed amount per order.

**VolumeImpactSlippage.** Slippage scales with order size relative to available volume. Larger orders in illiquid markets get worse fills. This is the most realistic model for anything beyond small caps.

And if none of these fit, you subclass `SlippageModel` and implement `calculate_slippage`:

```python
class MySlippage(SlippageModel):
    def calculate_slippage(self, price, order_side, amount=None):
        # Your logic here
        ...
```

## Commission models

Same story for fees. Three built-in options:

- **NoCommission.** Zero fees (default).
- **PercentageCommission.** Fee as a percentage of trade value.
- **FixedCommission.** Flat fee per trade.

And the same extensibility. Subclass `CommissionModel` for tiered pricing, maker/taker splits, or whatever your broker charges:

```python
class TieredCommission(CommissionModel):
    def calculate_commission(self, price, amount, order_side):
        trade_value = price * amount
        if trade_value > 10000:
            return trade_value * 0.0005  # 0.05% for large trades
        return trade_value * 0.001      # 0.1% for small trades
```

## Fill models and partial fills

This is where it gets interesting. Real markets don't always fill your entire order. If you're trading 10,000 shares and the candle only had 50,000 volume, you can't assume a full fill.

The `FillModel` controls how much of an order gets filled per evaluation:

- **FullFill.** Everything fills immediately (default).
- **VolumeBasedFill.** Fill amount is capped at a percentage of the candle's volume.

```python
app.set_blotter(SimulationBlotter(
    fill_model=VolumeBasedFill(max_volume_pct=0.1)  # max 10% of candle volume
))
```

With `VolumeBasedFill`, an order might take multiple candles to fully fill. The framework tracks `filled` and `remaining` amounts on each order, re-evaluating on every new candle until the order is complete.

## Transactions

Every fill gets recorded as a `Transaction`, a detailed record of what actually happened:

```python
transactions = context.get_transactions()
for tx in transactions:
    print(f"{tx.symbol} {tx.order_side}: "
          f"price={tx.price}, amount={tx.amount}, "
          f"commission={tx.commission}, slippage={tx.slippage}")
```

Each transaction captures: `order_id`, `symbol`, `order_side`, the actual fill `price` (after slippage), `amount`, total `cost`, `commission`, `slippage`, and `timestamp`. This gives you a complete audit trail for analyzing execution quality.

## Batch ordering

The blotter also supports batch ordering, placing multiple orders in a single call:

```python
context.batch_order([
    {"target_symbol": "BTC", "order_side": "BUY", "price": 50000, "amount": 0.1},
    {"target_symbol": "ETH", "order_side": "BUY", "price": 3000, "amount": 1.0},
])
```

The default implementation places orders sequentially. Override `batch_order()` in a custom blotter for atomic batch behavior or smart order routing across venues.

## What changed under the hood

This wasn't a small change: 2,161 additions and 724 deletions across 20 files. The key architectural shifts:

- **All orders now route through the blotter.** Previously, `Context` called `OrderService.create()` directly. Now every `create_order()`, `create_limit_order()`, and `create_market_order()` call goes through `self._blotter.place_order()`.
- **Removed `BacktestOrderExecutor`.** The old approach used a separate order executor for backtesting. The blotter replaces this. `OrderBacktestService` handles execution directly, and the `SimulationBlotter` provides the simulation behavior.
- **Stop-loss and take-profit orders route through the blotter.** The `TradeOrderEvaluator` now uses `self._create_order()` which delegates to the blotter, ensuring SL/TP fills are also subject to slippage and commission.
- **43 unit tests** covering all models, edge cases, partial fills, and the full integration path.

## Why this matters

If you're building a trading strategy, the gap between backtest performance and live performance is called _slippage and fees_. Most frameworks either ignore this or make it hard to configure.

With the blotter system, you can:

- Run a backtest with realistic slippage and commission assumptions
- Compare results across different fee structures
- Model partial fills in illiquid markets
- Build custom order routing logic without touching framework internals
- Audit every single fill after the fact
